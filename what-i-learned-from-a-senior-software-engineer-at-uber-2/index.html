Time Management: What I learned from the Head of Product Marketing at Google

As we're in the midst of the holiday season (or the tail end), I'm sure many of you planned on getting things done during your "time off" from your job, school, life, etc. I, for one, planned on spending this week learning more about the intricacies of Node and React, doing a bit of writing, and putting together a presentation.

Let me tell you right now that I've done none of that.

So, instead of mourning my loss of time, please allow me to be a bit more productive and tell you what I should've done and will do from now on based with some key points from this article featuring the Head of Product Marketing at Google, Jeremiah Dillon.

If you don't manage your time, someone else will do it for you.

-Some really smart person

It's true; someone else will. I've seen this happen countless of times at my previous job. If you didn't consciously block out slots on your calendar, you were assumed to be free for meetings, drop-ins, etc.

Jeremiah refers to two types of people: the manager and the maker. If you're managing people, it goes to without saying you're the former. If you're creating something (a product, a story, etc.), you're the latter.

As coders, we're makers. We're either coding something for ourselves or for our employers (or both).

So pay attention to the following...

The maker's day is different. They need to make, to create, to build. But, before that, they need to think.

Did you get that? We need time to think before we make, create, and build. And even then, we not only need to pay attention to how we allocate our time throughout the day, but throughout the week.

Here's how Jeremiah recommends splitting up your week:

  • Monday: set goals, organize, plan
  • Tuesday: heavy brainstorming and hard lifting, build if you have time
  • Wednesday: build, build, build
  • Thursday: any relevant meetings based off build time
  • Friday: open-ended work, socialize, plan long-term

Now that you have a general idea on how to split up your week, here's how I recommend splitting your day:

Mornings:

  • Protect them; this time should be spent first doing your most difficult and important work.
  • Create some sort of morning ritual you look forward to (mine is to get a cup of coffee / tea at around 10am).
  • When you reach a roadblock, get up and walk around for a bit.

In general:

  • Don't overschedule; calculate TTC(time to completion). It's better to finish everything on your list than create one too long and not finish any of it.
  • Save meetings for the end of the day if possible.
  • If you're a coffee drinker, take a look at this and try drinking a cup when your cortisol levels aren't already giving you that boost.
  • Finally, monitor your energy levels throughout the day for 2 weeks. You may be surprised to see there are certain hours where you feel "more productive" than others. Block this time for maker work.

I'm not going to lie, I feel much more productive writing this out. Hope you feel the same reading it.
